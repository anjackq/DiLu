# Draft 1: DiLu-Ollama Fine-Tuning Paper

## Working Title

Fine-Tuning Local LLM Driving Agents Under Runtime Constraints: A Three-Tier Highway Benchmark with DiLu-Ollama

## Abstract

Cloud-style evaluation can hide a central difficulty of local LLM agents: model quality and runtime viability are entangled when latency, timeout escalation, and fallback behavior directly affect whether an experiment remains comparable. We present DiLu-Ollama, a local-first extension of the DiLu driving-agent setting built on `highway-env`, Ollama-served models, a task-conditioned highway benchmark, and a publication-facing analysis pipeline \cite{wen2023dilu,leurent2018highwayenv}. Using this framework, we study whether fine-tuning improves local driving agents across lightweight, midclass, and highclass model tiers while treating benchmark validity as a first-class outcome rather than a hidden preprocessing filter. We find that lightweight is the only evidence-ready tier, that the Phi lightweight family provides the only exact ranking-eligible base-vs-fine-tuned comparison, and that all tested 14B models fail ranking eligibility under the current local timeout regime. These results support a sharper conclusion than a leaderboard alone would allow: fine-tuning can improve local driving agents, but those gains are only credible when benchmark validity survives the runtime constraints of local deployment.

## 1. Introduction

Large language models are increasingly used as decision-making components in autonomous-driving simulation, including recent systems that treat driving as a language-conditioned planning or action-selection problem \cite{wen2023dilu,sha2023languagempc}. That line of work is appealing because it offers a common interface for reasoning, instruction following, memory, and adaptation. Yet most of the field still reasons about these agents through task behavior alone: whether the car changes lanes correctly, avoids collisions, or completes a scenario. For local deployment, that view is incomplete. Once inference is moved onto commodity hardware and a fixed runtime budget, task quality and runtime feasibility become inseparable. The same agent can appear stronger or weaker depending not only on its policy quality, but on whether latency, timeout escalation, or fallback behavior prevent the run from remaining comparable at all. Prior work on consumer-grade LLM serving already shows that runtime constraints can dominate what is practically usable outside managed infrastructure \cite{song2023powerinfer}. In local autonomous-driving evaluation, they also determine which empirical conclusions survive scrutiny.

A benchmark for local LLM driving agents should therefore do more than rank completed trajectories. It should also expose when the evaluation itself begins to break down. This point matters especially for fine-tuning. Methods such as QLoRA make it practical to adapt local language models on limited hardware \cite{dettmers2023qlora}, but an adapted driving model is only meaningfully better if its gain survives the local runtime regime used to measure it. A task-only leaderboard cannot capture that distinction. If fine-tuning improves action quality but pushes a model into frequent timeout collapse, then the scientific conclusion is not a straightforward fine-tuning win; it is a tradeoff between policy quality and benchmark validity.

DiLu-Ollama addresses this measurement problem by combining a DiLu-style driving decision loop with local Ollama inference, `highway-env` simulation, a LaMPilot-style task-conditioned highway benchmark, and a post-hoc study pipeline that preserves invalidity and failure context rather than hiding it \cite{wen2023dilu,leurent2018highwayenv}. In this paper, we use that pipeline to study whether fine-tuning improves small-to-mid local driving models under a shared three-tier highway benchmark. The paper is ambitious in its framing but conservative in its evidence: it centers on the highway benchmark only, treats cross-scenario support as future scope, and restricts pairwise fine-tuning claims to exact ranking-eligible base-vs-fine-tuned pairs. Under those rules, the strongest positive evidence appears in lightweight, where the Phi family yields one exact eligible improvement. Midclass remains only partially usable for family-level interpretation, and highclass turns into a meaningful result of its own: a demonstration that the benchmark regime becomes operationally fragile enough at 14B scale that competitive ranking claims are no longer justified.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it presents a local-first evaluation and analysis scaffold for LLM driving agents in which benchmark validity is treated as part of the empirical outcome. Second, it provides a three-tier study showing that practical benchmark viability degrades with model tier under a fixed local runtime policy, making validity itself a central result rather than an afterthought. Third, it provides initial but non-trivial evidence that fine-tuning can improve local driving agents in lightweight settings, while showing exactly where broader family-level claims remain blocked.

## 2. Related Work

Recent work has established that large language models can act as high-level decision components for autonomous driving, but the literature is still split between papers that emphasize behavioral capability and papers that emphasize interpretability or system design. Early work such as *Drive Like a Human* argued that language models can serve as driving-policy interfaces rather than pure text processors \cite{fu2023drivelikehuman}. Follow-up systems including DiLu, LanguageMPC, and DriveGPT4 broadened that direction by treating language models as planners, decision makers, or interpretable end-to-end controllers in simulated driving settings \cite{wen2023dilu,sha2023languagempc,xu2023drivegpt4}. A recent survey, *LLM4Drive*, makes clear that this area is expanding quickly across planning, perception-language interaction, and embodied control \cite{yang2023llm4drive}. Our paper is closest to this line of work, but differs in emphasis: we do not primarily ask whether an LLM driving agent can complete a scenario. We ask when the local evaluation regime remains valid enough for those comparisons to be trusted.

That distinction also changes how we use the fine-tuning literature. Parameter-efficient adaptation methods such as LoRA and QLoRA make it feasible to specialize local language models without full retraining \cite{hu2021lora,dettmers2023qlora}. Systems work on local inference, including PowerInfer, further shows that practical deployment quality depends heavily on hardware-aware runtime behavior rather than model capability in isolation \cite{song2023powerinfer}. DiLu-Ollama sits at the intersection of these threads. It studies fine-tuned local driving agents, but evaluates them through a benchmark that preserves invalid runs, timeout collapse, and latency-induced comparability failures. In that sense, the paper is not only about driving-agent quality; it is about the conditions under which local fine-tuning claims remain scientifically interpretable.

## 3. Benchmark and Framework Setup

DiLu-Ollama extends the DiLu driving-agent loop into a local-first evaluation stack built on Ollama inference, `highway-env`, and a benchmark-oriented reporting pipeline \cite{wen2023dilu,leurent2018highwayenv}. For this paper, we use only the LaMPilot-style highway benchmark path. Each run produces task metrics such as `driving_score_v2`, task completion, and crash rate, together with latency and ranking-eligibility metadata. We treat that eligibility signal as part of the measurement outcome, because invalid runs in the current setup are often direct consequences of timeout escalation and latency collapse rather than random noise. Table~\ref{tab:validity-summary} therefore summarizes not only performance but also whether comparison remains methodologically sound across tiers.

The repository now also contains discrete cross-scenario support for merge and intersection environments, but those results are outside the evidential scope of this paper. All empirical claims below are tied to the three-tier highway study bundle under `analysis/out/publication_three_tier_results_v1/`.

## 4. Fine-Tuning Setup

The study compares base and fine-tuned models by family and tier using the registry and post-hoc analysis pipeline. We make pairwise fine-tuning claims only when both sides of a family remain ranking-eligible under the same benchmark regime. This is a strict rule, but it is necessary: otherwise a nominal base-versus-tuned comparison can collapse into an artifact of invalid execution rather than adaptation quality.

Under the current publication bundle, the only exact eligible pair is the lightweight Phi family. The paper should therefore be read as an initial fine-tuning study with narrow but real causal evidence, not as a complete family-by-family adaptation benchmark. The underlying repository pipeline supports local collection, training, export, and serving, and is compatible with efficient methods in the QLoRA line \cite{dettmers2023qlora}. In this paper, however, that training path is background context; the contribution is the empirical interpretation of fine-tuned models under local runtime constraints.

## 5. Experimental Design

We evaluate lightweight, midclass, and highclass models under a shared highway benchmark and restrict comparative claims to ranking-eligible rows. This separates two questions that local LLM papers often blur together: how well a model drives when a run is valid, and how often the local runtime policy allows that run to remain valid at all. Table~\ref{tab:validity-summary} and Figure~\ref{fig:cross-tier-validity} capture that screening layer explicitly.

Our headline task metric is `driving_score_v2`, reported alongside task completion, crash rate, and mean decision latency. We do not collapse these into a single global score, and we do not rank invalid runs by latency alone. Instead, we use validity to determine where comparative interpretation is justified. The main questions are therefore straightforward: which tier offers usable evidence, whether any exact eligible fine-tuning pairs improve, and how validity changes with model scale.

## 6. Results

### 6.1 Three-tier screening overview

The first result is not a ranking but a validity boundary. Table~\ref{tab:validity-summary} shows that 7 of 14 lightweight models remain ranking-eligible, compared with 3 of 6 midclass models and 0 of 4 highclass models. Figure~\ref{fig:cross-tier-validity} makes the same pattern visually explicit: as model tier increases, the benchmark stops behaving like a stable ranking instrument and starts behaving like a runtime stress test. This is the paper’s first substantive finding. Fine-tuning cannot be interpreted independently of this screening structure, because the benchmark only supports strong pairwise conclusions inside the region where runs remain valid. Under the current local runtime policy, that region is concentrated in lightweight.

### 6.2 Lightweight comparative evidence

Lightweight is the only tier where the benchmark supports both comparative ranking and exact pairwise interpretation. Table~\ref{tab:lightweight-leaderboard} shows that Llama 3.2 3B Base is the top valid lightweight model in the current bundle, reaching `driving_score_v2=0.1403` with a task completion rate of `0.35` at roughly `1196` ms mean decision latency. Several fine-tuned lightweight models also remain competitive, notably DiLu Qwen3 1.7B v1 and DiLu Phi-4 Mini 3.8B Instruct v1. Figure~\ref{fig:cross-tier-pareto} is useful here because it shows that the lightweight story is not just about peak score; it is about occupying the only part of the latency-quality frontier where multiple local models remain both valid and interpretable. In other words, lightweight is not merely the smallest tier in the paper. It is the only tier where the benchmark currently behaves like the kind of comparative instrument the fine-tuning question actually needs.

### 6.3 Exact fine-tuning evidence in lightweight

The strongest exact fine-tuning result appears in the Phi family. Table~\ref{tab:lightweight-pair-delta} shows that DiLu Phi-4 Mini 3.8B Instruct v1 improves `driving_score_v2` by `0.0419` and `overall_score_mean` by `0.1006` relative to the base Phi-4 Mini 3.8B model, while also reducing mean decision latency by about `106` ms. This is important because it clears the paper’s strictest evidential bar: both sides of the comparison are ranking-eligible under the same local runtime regime. That makes the Phi pair more than an anecdote. It is direct evidence that fine-tuning can improve a local driving agent without paying an obvious latency penalty. At the same time, the same table also defines the limit of the claim. The paper can argue for a real lightweight fine-tuning effect, but it cannot generalize that effect across families that never become jointly rankable.

### 6.4 Midclass screening results

Midclass shows why screening evidence is still scientifically useful even when pairwise claims remain blocked. The best valid midclass model is DiLu DeepSeek R1 8B v1 with `driving_score_v2=0.1130`, and Table~\ref{tab:midclass-screening} shows that every midclass family still fails the exact eligible-pair criterion somewhere in the base-versus-fine-tuned comparison. The correct interpretation is therefore not that midclass is uninformative, but that it reveals partial viability without clean causal comparison. Some 8B models can survive the benchmark and produce meaningful scores, yet the tier as a whole remains too brittle for the paper to claim a family-level fine-tuning effect. That is a stronger methodological point than simply saying the evidence is missing: the benchmark is actively showing where interpretation stops being secure.

### 6.5 Highclass failure analysis

Highclass turns the benchmark from a ranking tool into a failure-analysis instrument. None of the four tested 14B models are ranking-eligible in the current bundle, and Table~\ref{tab:highclass-failure} shows that the dominant failure mode is timeout-driven collapse combined with incomplete execution. Even the least latent highclass failure, DiLu Qwen3 14B v1, still averages roughly `17.9` seconds per decision. Figure~\ref{fig:cross-tier-pareto} reflects the consequence directly: highclass contributes no valid points to the main frontier. This is not absent evidence; it is a meaningful empirical result. Under the current local runtime regime, the benchmark itself reveals a scale boundary beyond which comparative claims about fine-tuning or model quality are no longer trustworthy. That failure mode strengthens the paper’s main argument by showing that runtime validity is not a secondary reporting detail. It is part of what defines whether the benchmark is measuring policy quality at all.

## 7. Limitations

The main limitation of the current paper is sparse exact pair coverage. Although the repository supports many model families and multiple tiers, only one exact base-vs-fine-tuned pair is ranking-eligible in the current evidence bundle. This means the fine-tuning story is real but still narrow. The paper should be explicit that family-level generalization remains unresolved.

The second limitation is scope. The repository contains cross-scenario infrastructure for merge and intersection tasks, but those settings are not part of the empirical evidence in the present manuscript. The first paper should therefore remain highway-only and avoid implying any cross-scenario generalization. A third limitation is runtime regime specificity: the current timeout ladder and local hardware context are part of the studied setup. The paper can describe them as a realistic local-first regime, but not as a universal benchmark standard.

## 8. Conclusion

DiLu-Ollama makes local-first LLM driving evaluation more scientifically usable by combining simulation, task-conditioned benchmarking, runtime-aware validity handling, and publication-facing analysis. When we apply this pipeline to a three-tier highway study, the strongest comparative evidence comes from lightweight models, while midclass remains screening-only and highclass primarily reveals runtime-scalability failure under the current timeout policy. Within that evidence base, the Phi lightweight pair provides a concrete example that fine-tuning can improve local driving performance without increasing mean latency. At the same time, the current study is intentionally conservative: broader family-level fine-tuning claims remain blocked until more exact pairs become ranking-eligible. That combination of positive evidence and explicit failure visibility is the paper’s core value.
